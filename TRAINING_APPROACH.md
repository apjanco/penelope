# How We Train the Model

## What we're trying to do

We want a small, local language model that can read a passage from a novel and reliably
answer two questions: *Is this stream of consciousness?* and, if so, *which kind?*

"Stream of consciousness" is not one thing. Robert Humphrey and Erwin Steinberg identified
at least ten distinct techniques — direct interior monologue, free indirect discourse,
reverie, free association, and others — each of which works differently on a reader and
signals something different about narrative form. A model that lumps them together is not
much use for literary analysis. So the task is harder than a yes/no classifier, and the
correct answer often requires genuine interpretive reasoning, not just pattern matching.

The model we fine-tune is **Qwen3-4B** — small enough to run on a single consumer GPU,
capable enough to handle nuanced literary prose. Our goal is to make it a reliable
specialist, not a general-purpose oracle.

---

## Where the training data comes from

We do not have a large hand-labeled dataset. Labeling stream of consciousness passages
at scale, with consistent application of a ten-category taxonomy, would require
significant expert time. Instead, we use a process called **silver labeling**:

1. We assemble a corpus of texts where stream of consciousness is prominent — works by
   Joyce, Woolf, Faulkner, Richardson, Schnitzler, and others — and a separate corpus
   of Gutenberg texts with predominantly externalized narration (Conan Doyle, Dickens,
   Trollope, Twain) as negative examples.

2. We run several large, capable frontier LLMs (e.g., GPT-5, Gemini) on every chunk of
   the positive corpus, asking each model to identify and classify every SoC instance
   using our taxonomy.

3. We apply **consensus filtering**: a training example is included only if a majority
   of models agree on the classification. This removes noisy or idiosyncratic labels
   and leaves us with examples where multiple independent, capable models converged on
   the same answer — a reasonable proxy for correctness.

The result is a few hundred high-quality training examples in
`dataset/train.jsonl`, each structured as a conversation turn where the model is shown
a passage and is expected to produce a `<think>` reasoning trace followed by a
structured JSON classification.

---

## Two stages of training

### Stage 1: Supervised fine-tuning (SFT)

We fine-tune Qwen3-4B using **QLoRA** — a technique that trains a small set of
additional weight matrices (the "adapter") rather than the full model. This is far
more memory-efficient than full fine-tuning and produces a model that is easy to
share, version, and update.

The training objective here is simple: learn to produce outputs that look like the
silver-labeled examples. The model learns both the *reasoning trace* (what to notice
about a passage before committing to a label) and the *structured JSON output*. Learning
the reasoning trace matters because it forces the model to produce interpretable
intermediate steps, not just a label. A model that can explain *why* it classified
something is more trustworthy and easier to correct.

This gives us a base that is far better than zero-shot Qwen3-4B on this task, but the
silver labels have a ceiling: the training data was generated by other LLMs, so the
model is partly learning to imitate their tendencies, not necessarily to be right.

### Stage 2: Reinforcement learning from an interpretive judge (GRPO)

To push past the silver-label ceiling, we apply **GRPO** (Group Relative Policy
Optimization) — a reinforcement learning technique in which the model is scored on
the quality of its outputs and learns to favor responses that score higher.

The judge is a much larger model, **Qwen3.6-27B**, running as a separate server. For
each passage in the training set, the fine-tuned model generates eight candidate
responses. All eight are scored, and the model is updated to make high-scoring
responses more likely and low-scoring responses less likely.

The score is a weighted blend of four components:

| Component | Weight | How it's measured |
|---|---|---|
| **Grounding** | 20% | Does the reasoning trace quote actual words from the passage? |
| **Skepticism** | 20% | Does the model consider and rule out alternative interpretations? |
| **Specificity** | 20% | Does the reasoning use precise taxonomy terms from our framework? |
| **Type coherence** | 40% | Does the chosen label actually fit the passage, per the judge? |

Two of these (grounding and specificity) are computed automatically without any API
call — we just check whether quoted phrases appear verbatim in the source text, and
whether known taxonomy terms appear in the reasoning. Two (skepticism and type
coherence) require the judge to read the response and score it.

The result is a model whose reasoning traces are not just fluent but *anchored*: they
quote the text, name the right terms, and show the interpretive work.

## How we know it's working

### Comparison against a held-out test set

The dataset is split into train / validation / test before any training begins. The
test set is never touched during training or hyperparameter decisions. After training,
we run `scripts/evaluate.py`, which reports per-class precision, recall, and F1 for
every taxonomy category, plus a confusion matrix.

We compare three things:
- **Zero-shot Qwen3-4B** — the base model with no fine-tuning at all
- **Silver majority-vote baseline** — the consensus of the frontier models that
  generated the training data
- **Fine-tuned Penelope** — our trained model

If fine-tuning is working, Penelope should substantially outperform zero-shot Qwen3-4B
and hold its own against (or surpass) the silver baseline on the categories where
silver agreement was strong.

### Watching the training dynamics

During GRPO training, we monitor two signals:

- **Mean reward** should increase over training steps, indicating the model is learning
  to produce better-scored responses.
- **KL divergence** (a measure of how far the model has drifted from its SFT starting
  point) should remain moderate. A sudden spike in KL divergence is a sign of reward
  hacking — the model has found a way to score well without actually improving (e.g.,
  by producing very long reasoning traces that superficially match grounding checks).
  We stop or adjust training if this happens.

### What "working" means for this task

Because SoC classification is an interpretive act — scholars genuinely disagree about
edge cases — we do not expect near-perfect accuracy. What we do expect:

- **Recall on clear cases is high.** Direct interior monologue and free association
  with strong formal markers should be found reliably.
- **Confusions make sense.** When the model misclassifies, it should confuse adjacent
  categories (e.g., indirect interior monologue with direct interior monologue) rather
  than making category errors (e.g., calling a clearly externalized narration passage
  stream of consciousness).
- **Reasoning is legible and anchored.** A literary scholar reading the model's
  explanation should be able to follow its reasoning and identify where they agree or
  disagree — even when the final label is wrong.

The last criterion is harder to automate but is ultimately what makes the tool useful
for research. An opaque classifier that is sometimes right is less valuable than one
whose reasoning can be checked, corrected, and trusted.
